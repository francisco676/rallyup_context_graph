The Core Idea

What Is a Context Graph?

A context graph is a set of structured markdown files that describe who you are, what you know, and what your company sells. No fancy tools, no subscriptions. Just organized information any AI can read.

In the age of AI, good context = good content. The prompt is almost irrelevant. The context is everything.

When you hand a generic prompt to Claude, you get generic content. When you hand it a context graph, it knows your expertise, your voice, your audience, your goals, and what you should never say. The output stops sounding like AI and starts sounding like you.

The Architecture

The 3 Layers of a Strong Context Graph

Every effective context graph is built in three layers. Each one makes AI output sharper.

L1
Professional Identity
Your role, your expertise, your ideal audience, and your LinkedIn goals. This is the foundation. Without it, the AI does not know who it is writing for or why.
L2
Voice DNA
Your writing style, tone, sentence patterns, words you use, words you avoid. This is what makes your content sound like you instead of like every other executive on LinkedIn.
L3
Content Library
Past posts, what performed well, ongoing themes. The AI gets smarter every time you add a post to this layer. Your best content trains future content.

The Build Guide

Step-by-Step: Fill In Your Context Graph in 45 Minutes

Open Claude and work through these five steps in order. The starter kit files have placeholder sections that map exactly to each step. Each prompt is ready to paste.

Step 1 Your Company Context (15 min) — 5 files

Paste this into Claude with your company name and website. It will research your public presence and generate five structured files. Fix anything marked [NEEDS VERIFICATION] manually.

Prompt to paste into Claude
Research my company and create 5 structured markdown files. For each, include "Connected Nodes" at the bottom with [[wikilinks]]. My company: [YOUR COMPANY NAME] Website: [YOUR WEBSITE URL] 1. positioning.md — value prop, 3-4 differentiators, public tone Connected Nodes: [[icps]], [[product]], [[competitors]], [[trends]] 2. icps.md — 2-3 ideal customer profiles, pain points, evaluation criteria Connected Nodes: [[positioning]], [[product]], [[trends]] 3. product.md — products/services, key features, who uses them Connected Nodes: [[positioning]], [[icps]], [[competitors]] 4. competitors.md — 3-5 competitors, where you win, where they win Connected Nodes: [[positioning]], [[product]], [[trends]] 5. trends.md — 5-7 current trends, why each matters for my company Connected Nodes: [[positioning]], [[competitors]] Label anything uncertain as [NEEDS VERIFICATION].

Save into the company/ and market/ folders from your kit.

Step 2 Your Voice (10 min) — the most important file

Paste your five best LinkedIn posts alongside this prompt. If you have no posts yet, use the alternative below.

Prompt to paste into Claude (with existing posts)
Analyze my LinkedIn posts and create a brand-voice.md file. Include: - Core Identity: who I am professionally (1-2 sentences) - Voice Archetype: one sentence description of how I write - DO list: 8-10 things I consistently do in my writing - DON'T list: 8-10 things I avoid - Signature Phrases: 5-10 direct quotes from my posts - Vocabulary: 10-15 words I use / 10-15 to avoid - How I Open Posts: pattern + 3 examples - How I Close Posts: pattern + 3 examples Connected Nodes: [[guardrails]], [[sales-focus]], [[topic-1]] Here are my posts: [PASTE YOUR 5-10 BEST LINKEDIN POSTS HERE]
Alternative — no posts yet
Help me define my brand voice. Ask me these 5 questions one at a time, then create brand-voice.md from my answers: 1. What's your role and what makes your perspective unique? 2. How do people describe your communication style at work? 3. Name 2-3 LinkedIn creators whose tone you admire. 4. What kind of posts make you cringe? 5. Data-driven or story-driven? Formal or casual? Teaching or sharing?

Save as: voice/brand-voice.md

Step 3 Your Expertise Topics (10 min) — 2-3 concept files

These files define the topics you are credible to post about. Each one becomes a content engine the AI draws from when building posts.

Prompt to paste into Claude
Based on my role as [YOUR ROLE] at [YOUR COMPANY], define 2-3 content topics for my LinkedIn presence. For each, create a separate markdown file with: - Definition: what this topic means to me (2-3 sentences) - What It Means: 3-4 bullet points on what I believe - What It Does Not Mean: 2-3 misconceptions to avoid - Company Connection: how this relates to what my company does - Content Ideas: 5 specific post ideas Connected Nodes: [[brand-voice]], [[positioning]], [[sales-focus]] My expertise areas: [TOPIC 1]: [one sentence] [TOPIC 2]: [one sentence]

Save as: concepts/topic-name.md

Step 4 Guardrails and Goals (5 min)

Two short files that protect your voice and keep content on strategy.

Prompt to paste into Claude
Create two files for my LinkedIn content strategy: 1. guardrails.md Topics I should never post about: - [List 3-5: e.g. politics, competitor bashing, hustle culture] Patterns that underperform on LinkedIn: - Generic advice without a specific story - Posts that end with "What do you think?" - Abstract predictions without personal experience - Pure self-promotion disguised as insight Connected Nodes: [[brand-voice]], [[sales-focus]] 2. sales-focus.md My LinkedIn goals, ranked: 1. [e.g. build personal authority] 2. [e.g. generate inbound leads] Content priorities in order: 1. [e.g. real stories from work] 2. [e.g. industry observations] Connected Nodes: [[brand-voice]], [[guardrails]], [[topic-1]]
Step 5 The Master Index (5 min)

This file tells any AI exactly how to use your graph when you ask it to write a post. Think of it as the operating manual for your whole system.

Prompt to paste into Claude
Create my-context-graph.md as a master index file. Include: Identity: one paragraph — name, role, company, what makes me distinct Node Map: list every file with a one-line description and wikilink. Group into: Voice / Concepts / Company / Market Format: - [[filename]] — one line description Execution Instructions: numbered list for writing a post: 1. Check [[guardrails]] first 2. Find a specific story or observation to anchor the post 3. Match the topic to a concept node 4. Read [[brand-voice]] for voice rules 5. Check [[sales-focus]] — confirm this serves a ranked goal 6. Write in my voice: open with experience, let insight emerge 7. End with a declaration or reflection, not a question Connected Nodes: [link to every file]

Save as: my-context-graph.md in the root folder.

Using Your Graph

How to Write a Post With Your Context Graph

Once your files are filled in, use this master prompt in Claude. Even 3 files will produce dramatically better output than a blank prompt.

The master content prompt
Read all the files in my context graph (pasted below). Write a LinkedIn post about [YOUR TOPIC]. Follow the execution instructions in my-context-graph.md. Match my voice from brand-voice.md. Check guardrails.md before writing. [PASTE: my-context-graph.md] [PASTE: brand-voice.md] [PASTE: guardrails.md] [PASTE: relevant concept file]

Tips That Make the Graph Compound Over Time

  • Update your graph quarterly. Trends change. Your goals shift. Your voice evolves. Set a 90-day reminder.
  • Save every published post to the content/ folder. Each one trains future content.
  • The graph compounds. The first post will be good. The tenth will be indistinguishable from you.
  • Share it across your team. Multiple people can share the same company/ and market/ files and only need individual voice and concept files.
  • Use Obsidian to visualize your graph. Open your folder as a vault, press Cmd+G, and see every file and every [[wikilink]] as a visual node map.
